Theme 5 · History & Methods of Psychology

The Demarcation Debate: science vs. pseudo-science

how the 20th century tried to define science — and watched the rulebook collapse
Roots: empiricism & positivism The demarcation problem Logical positivism The problems Popper Kuhn & Lakatos
Theme 4 asked how scientific knowledge is built and justified — induction, the empirical cycle, the ethos of science. Theme 5 asks the harder question: what actually separates real science from pseudo-science? That is the demarcation problem — and this map follows the 20th-century attempt to solve it rise, crack, and fall apart.
1
The roots: empiricism & positivismLocke · Hume · Comte · Mach — pioneers of the philosophy of science

Before any demarcation rule, two traditions set the stage: empiricism (all knowledge comes from experience) and positivism (only observable, verifiable facts count — reject metaphysics).

EmpiricistJohn Locke

Mind at birth is a tabula rasa (blank slate); no innate ideas. We know only our ideas of things, not their essence — representationalism.

Primary qualities resemble objects; secondary qualities (colour) are produced in us.

EmpiricistDavid Hume

All we have are impressions and ideas. Causation is only constant conjunction + succession; "necessity" is a habit of mind, not observed.

The Problem of Induction: no number of cases gives certainty about the next.

PositivistAuguste Comte

Three stages of knowledge: theological → metaphysical → positive. The positive stage abandons first causes and rests on observation alone.

Coined the positivist programme; aligned with common-sense realism.

PositivistErnst Mach

The world is made of sensations / "elements." Laws are just economical descriptions of how sensations relate — no hidden causes needed.

Drove the "unity of science" idea; influenced Skinner & the Vienna Circle.
2
The demarcation problemthe question that drives the whole theme

The central quest of the philosophy of science: find a demarcation criterion — a universal, ahistoric, algorithmic rule that separates genuine science from pseudo-science and metaphysics.

WhatA universal yardstick

A method for rationality applied algorithmically, leaving nothing to taste, judgement, or social/historical factors.

WhyWhy it mattered

To measure progress (why Einstein > Newton) and to keep out quacks and ideologies — the Vienna Circle worked under the rising Nazi threat.

3
Logical Positivism — the "Standard View"Vienna Circle (Wiener Kreis) · verifiability · unified science

The dominant philosophy of science, 1920s–60s. Its answer to demarcation: verifiability.

CoreVerifiability = meaning

The meaning of a statement is its method of verification. If you can't say how to verify it, it is literally nonsense.

"Water boils at 100°C" = meaningful; "God is love" = meaningless (can't even be false).

FixCorrespondence rules

Unobservables (electron, intelligence) are admitted only if linked to observations via correspondence rules.

"Intelligence = a score on an IQ test."

GoalUnified science

Theories are logical structures (deductive-nomological). All sciences reduce to physics; progress is cumulative.

Justification matters, not discovery (coffee or jogging is irrelevant).

Assumptions of the Standard View (lecture 3)

Sense data are the basis Unobservables need observation links Theories are logical & linguistic Sciences unify (reduce to physics) Only justification counts Science is cumulative

Carnap's softer move: replace strict verification with confirmation — a degree of probability from 0 to 1. (The logic of induction never worked.)

4
The problems — collapse from withinespecially: there is no neutral, theory-free observation

The most vulnerable assumption was that neutral observation is possible. Four attacks — mostly from positivism's own allies — broke it.

SellarsMyth of the Given

There is no incorrigible "given" in the mind. The myth of Jones: even reports of our own mental states are theory-laden and fallible.

QuineTwo Dogmas

No clean analytic/synthetic split; holism — theories face "the tribunal of experience" only as a corporate body, never one statement alone.

Quine–DuhemUnderdetermination

Any statement can be held true by adjusting the rest of the system. Data underdetermine theory — so verification isn't a clean test.

HansonTheory-ladenness

Kepler and Tycho watch the same dawn but see different things. "Seeing is theory-laden" — observation already carries a framework.

Result: if there is no theory-neutral observation, verification and the whole demarcation project are in deep trouble.

5
Popper — demarcation revived by falsificationconjectures & refutations · critical vs dogmatic thinking

Popper saves demarcation by dropping verification. His key logical point: you can never verify "all swans are white," but a single black swan refutes it.

CritiqueAgainst verification

General laws cover infinite cases → can never be verified. Confirmation can't be measured. Only falsification is logically certain (modus tollens).

CriterionFalsificationism

Demarcation = falsifiability. A good theory forbids things; its empirical content = how much it rules out. Make bold conjectures, then try to refute them.

AttitudeCritical vs dogmatic

Science = ruthless criticism; pseudo-science = dogmatic, unfalsifiable systems that explain everything.

Marx & Freud: post-hoc explanations for anything → not science.
Problems with falsification: real scientists don't drop a theory at the first failure — they add ad hoc rescues; and since observation is theory-laden, there are no neutral "falsifying facts." Strict falsification can't be the demarcation rule either.
6
Kuhn — demarcation abandoned: paradigmsnormal science · anomaly · crisis · revolution · incommensurability

Kuhn brings history into science. Criteria for rationality change with the paradigm — so there is no timeless demarcation rule.

Key ideaParadigm

Not just a theory: a whole framework of theories + methods + exemplars + equipment + community + worldview. It defines what counts as a legitimate fact.

Normal sciencePuzzle-solving

Within a paradigm, the framework is not criticised; research is puzzle-solving — filling in detail. Falsification would be counter-productive.

IncommensurabilityNo rational comparison

Rival paradigms are incommensurable — facts exist only inside a paradigm, so neutral facts can't decide between them. A shift is a Gestalt switch / conversion, won by persuasion.

7
Lakatos — saving rationality: research programmeshard core + protective belt · progressive vs degenerating

Lakatos tries to combine Kuhn with rationality — keeping relativism at bay through competition between research programmes.

StructureHard core + protective belt

A programme has an untouchable hard core (the negative heuristic) and a protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses that absorb anomalies.

TestEmpirical content

The winner is the programme whose empirical content increases — that predicts and finds novel facts.

Astronomy: predicting an unknown planet → Uranus is found → content grows.

VerdictProgressive vs degenerating

Progressive: opens new areas, anticipates new facts. Degenerating: survives only by ever-more ad hoc patches.

An a-posteriori "rational reconstruction" — not instant rationality.
Coda — Feyerabend (beyond your checklist): radicalises Kuhn into methodological anarchism — "anything goes," there should be no demarcation rule at all. The debate ends where it began: with no universal method.
